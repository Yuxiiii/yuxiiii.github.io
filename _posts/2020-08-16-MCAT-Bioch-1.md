## 1.1 Amino Acids Found in Proteins

Amino acids are molecules that contain two functional groups: an **amino group(-NH<sub>2</sub>)** and an **carboxyl group(-COOH)**. And between them there is a carbon atom acting as a bridge to connect these two group called alpha-carbon. In addition to the amino and carboxyl groups, the alpha-carbon has two other groups attached on it: a **hydrogen** and a **side chain, or called R group**. The R group determine the properties of amino acids, and therefore their functions.

### HYDROPHOBIC and HYDROPHILIC AMINO ACIDS

Firstly, the amino acids with alkyl side chains, such as alanine, valine, leucine, ioleucine, and phenylalanine are strongly hydrophobic and thus more likely to be found in the interior of proteins, away from water on the surface of the protein.

Secondly, all the amino acids with charged side chains including positively charged amino acids such as histidine, arginine, and lysine, plus negatively charged glutamate and aspartate are hydrophilic.

## 1.2 Acid-Base Chemistry of Amino Acids

Amino acids have both an acidic carboxylic acid group and a basic amino group, which makes them amphoteric species. The key to understanding the behavior of amino acids is to remember two facts:
- Ionizable groups tend to gain protons under acidic conditions and lose them under basic conditions. In other words, ionizable groups tend to be protonated at low pH, while they prone to be deprotonated at high pH.
- The p*K*<sub>a</sub> of a group is the pH at which, on average, half of the molecules of that species are deprotonated. 
<center> [protonated version of the ionizable group] = [deprotonated version of the ionizable group] </center>

<center> OR </center>

<center> [HA] = [A<sup>-</sup>] </center>

If the pH is less than p*K*<sub>a</sub>, a majority of the species will be protonated, while if the pH is higher than p*K*<sub>a</sub>, a majority of the species will be deprotonated.

### PROTONATION and DEPROTONATION
The first p*K*<sub>a</sub>, p*K*<sub>a1</sub>, represents the p*K*<sub>a</sub> for the carboxyl group, and it is usually around **2**. The second p*K*<sub>a</sub>, p*K*<sub>a2</sub>, is the p*K*<sub>a</sub> for the amino group for most amino acids, which is usually between **9 and 10**. And for amino acids with an ionizable side chain, there wukk be three p*K*<sub>a</sub> values.

#### Positively Charged under Acidic Conditions
At very low pH, both amino group and carboxyl group would be protonated to -NH<sub>3</sub><sup>+</sup> and -COOH. Therefore, under such acidic circumstance, amino acids tend to be positively charged.

#### Negatively Charged under Basic Conditions
While at high pH condition, both amino group and carboxyl group would be deprotonated tp -NH<sub>2</sub> and -COOH<sup>-</sup> which would make the whole amino acids tend to be negatively charged.

#### Zwitterions at Intermediate pH
As pH of the amino acid solution is around pH1 to pH 7.4, the normal pH of human blood, carboxylate group is deprotonated (-COO<sup>-</sup>) and amino group is protonated (NH<sub>3</sub><sup>+</sup>). And overall, the molecule is electrically neutral. Such molecules are called dipolar ions or **zwitterions**.

### TITRATION of AMINO ACIDS
As pH = p*K*<sub>a1</sub>, that is, [<sup>+</sup>NH<sub>3</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>COOH] = [<sup>+</sup>NH<sub>3</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>COOH]. In a similar way, as pH = p*K*<sub>a2</sub>, [<sup>+</sup>NH<sub>3</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>COO<sup>-</sup>] = [NH<sub>2</sub>CH<sub>2</sub>COO<sup>-</sup>]. And in such phase, the titration curve is nearby horizontal.

And as pH = pI = $\frac{p*K*<sub>a, NH<sub>3</sub><sup>+</sup></sub> + p*K*<sub>a, COOH</sub>}{2}$

## 1.3 Peptide Bond Formation and Hydrolysis

Peptides are composed of amino acid subunits, or called **residues**. **Dipeptides** consist of two amino acid residues; **tripeptides** have three. And the term **oligopeptide** is used for relatively small peptides which is up to 20 residues; while longer chains are called **polypeptides**

The reidues in peptides are joined together through **peptide bonds** (-C(O)NH-). In this section, the key reactions invovled in forming and breaking peptide bonds are discussed.
